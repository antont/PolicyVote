# Informed or Influenced? Voting Consistently in Direct Democratic Votes

**Author:** Giada Barbieri
**Presented:** Western Political Science Association (WPSA) 2024
**PDF:** https://www.wpsanet.org/papers/docs/WPSA%202024%20Barbieri.pdf

## Research Question

Barbieri asks whether ordinary citizens in direct democracy (California and Switzerland) **vote consistently with their own policy preferences**, and how **political knowledge** and **elite cues** (from parties, government, interest groups) help or hinder that consistency.

Three specific questions:
1. Do citizens vote "consistently" in direct democratic votes?
   - "Consistent voting" = casting a referendum vote that aligns with one's **substantive position on the policy arguments**, not just with party ID or ideology
2. What is the effect of political knowledge on consistent voting?
3. What is the effect of political cues on consistent voting?

## Methodology

Original survey data on **four direct democratic projects** (two in Switzerland, two in California).

## Key Findings

### Strong baseline consistency
- Citizens' **policy positions are a strong predictor** of how they vote in referendums
- There is substantial "consistent voting" even before adding knowledge and cues
- Survey vote distributions closely match official referendum results

### Political knowledge increases consistent voting
- Higher **issue knowledge** (knowing what the proposal does, understanding its pros and cons) is associated with **higher probability of voting consistently** with one's argument-based position
- Knowledgeable voters can better connect their evaluations of policy features to a clear Yes/No choice

### Cues can both help AND hurt consistency
- **When elite cues confirm** a person's argument-based position → **increase consistent voting**
- **When cues conflict** with a person's argument-based position → **reduce consistent voting**
- Cues **do not automatically make people more representative of their own preferences**

### Context matters: Switzerland vs California
- In **Switzerland** (government/parties lead): government and party recommendations matter more
- In **California** (interest groups dominate): interest group cues have more influence
- The relative weight of different cues varies across systems

## Conclusions about Citizens Voting Directly on Policies

1. **Direct democratic voting is not inherently "irrational" or random**
   - Many citizens are able to vote in line with their policy preferences
   - Policy positions are a strong predictor of referendum choices
   - Supports the **normative potential** of direct democracy

2. **Knowledge improves the democratic quality of referendum votes**
   - Higher issue-specific knowledge increases likelihood that votes genuinely reflect citizens' own preferences

3. **Cues are double-edged tools**
   - Can **facilitate representation** when they help voters translate predispositions into a choice
   - Can also **distort representation** when they lead citizens away from their own positions
   - Elite influence can undermine congruence between preferences and votes

4. **Information environment matters for direct democracy efficacy**
   - If citizens cannot align opinions with votes, efficacy is undermined
   - Campaign cues and information quality are crucial for whether direct democracy delivers representative outcomes

## Relevance to PolicyVote

This paper provides empirical support for PolicyVote's core premise:

1. **Citizens CAN vote consistently on policies** - contrary to democratic realist assumptions, Barbieri finds substantial consistent voting in referendums

2. **The deliberation phase matters** - PolicyVote's emphasis on quality deliberation before voting aligns with finding that knowledge improves consistency

3. **Elite cue effects are context-dependent** - PolicyVote's structure (where parties propose but don't dominate) could minimize harmful cue effects while preserving helpful ones

4. **Direct policy voting can work** - the paper explicitly notes that "citizens vote on **specific policies and not for candidates or parties**" and finds this can produce representative outcomes when information quality is high
